How TennisIQ Works

A 538-style ATP analytics platform. This page explains how the predictions, ratings, and percentile rankings are computed, and what the model doesn't know.

01What TennisIQ Is

TennisIQ predicts ATP match outcomes and surfaces unique player characteristics from 919,000 matches dating back to 1968. Predictions come from a stacked machine-learning ensemble. Player profiles draw on Glicko-2 ratings, shot-by-shot charted data, and percentile rankings across 17 career stats.

Three things make a player profile worth opening: how strong they are, how their game changes by surface, and what makes them unusual. Each page is built around those three questions.

02The Rating System

Ratings start with Glicko-2, a chess-style rating system that accounts for uncertainty (rating deviation, RD) and inactivity. We compute four ratings per player: overall, hard, clay, and grass.

The displayed 0–100 number is a sigmoid-mapped percentile of the underlying rating, with a small adjustment for recent form (the player's win rate over their last 3 matches, ±4 points max). The mapping puts the elite few at Legendary (91+), the deep tour pool at Gold (80–90), then Silver (69–79) and Bronze (<69) below.

Retired players show their peak career Glicko with a "Peak: YYYY" label, not their decayed current value. Active players show their current Glicko with the form modifier applied. We mark a player retired if 14+ months have passed since their last main-tour match (the threshold catches genuine retirements without flagging injured players).

03The Prediction Model

Match predictions come from a stacked ensemble with three layers:

  • XGBoost on 168 features (Brier 0.189 on holdout)
  • LightGBM on the same features (Brier 0.186)
  • Logistic regression meta-model trained on the two predictions plus calibration features (final Brier 0.184)

The 168 features include surface, tournament level, best-of, current Glicko on each surface, RD, age, recent form windows (3, 5, 15 matches), head-to-head record, fatigue proxies, and weather-conditioning features for outdoor matches.

Baseline Brier (always-pick-higher-Elo): 0.222 · TennisIQ ensemble: 0.184 · Pinnacle market reference: ~0.170

Training is chronological with a temporal split: the model only ever sees matches before the validation date when learning from them. Zero leakage.

04Confidence Intervals

The ± band on each prediction is derived from the model's Brier score on similar matches in the trailing 365-day back-test, bucketed by surface. A higher Brier in a bucket widens the band; more samples narrow it. The band is capped at 15 percentage points and never crosses 0% or 100%.

"Why this prediction" surfaces the top three numerical drivers (rating gap, surface fit, head-to-head, recent form, attribute mismatches) signed in favor of the predicted winner. Magnitudes are estimated percentage-point contributions, not exact SHAP values.

05The Percentile Engine

For every player with 20+ career main-tour matches, we compute 17 stats and rank them within the qualifying population. Stats fall into two categories.

Match-result stats (1968–today)

  • Tiebreak win rate, deciding-set win rate, 3-set match win rate
  • vs Top-10 and vs Top-20 win rate (opponent rank at match time)
  • Comeback rate (wins after losing first set), first-set conversion
  • Bagels delivered and conceded per match

Serve and return stats (1991+, where match-stat data exists)

  • Hold %, break %, BP save %, BP convert %
  • 1st-serve win %, 2nd-serve win %, aces per match, double faults per match

Each player's player page surfaces the five stats where they are most extreme — top 10% (a strength) or bottom 10% (a real weakness). Rankings update daily.

06Surface DNA

Each player's three surface ratings (hard, clay, grass) are compared to their overall rating. The differences classify their identity:

  • Specialist: 30+ rating points above overall on one surface
  • All-Court: within 15 points of overall on all three surfaces
  • Threat: solid baseline plus one surface where they meaningfully outperform

The narrative on each surface card draws on the attribute mix (clay leans on endurance and groundstroke; grass on serve and volley; hard on mental and clutch).

07Match Narrative Generator

The narrative on the compare page is template-based. It generates four paragraphs (setup, key matchup dynamic, surface factor, prediction) by selecting from analyst-style language conditioned on the prediction, the upset risk score, and the top driver.

An optional Claude-powered version produces freeform prose when an Anthropic API key is configured. The site currently runs the template version.

08Data Sources

  • Sackmann ATP repository: match results 1968–2024, ~195,000 main-tour matches with score, surface, ranks, and serve/return stats from 1991 onward (CC BY-NC-SA 4.0)
  • tennis-data.co.uk: scraped supplemental matches December 2024 through current (~3,300 matches with surface, indoor/outdoor, betting odds)
  • Match Charting Project: shot-by-shot data for ~7,200 matches across ~980 players. Powers volley, footwork, rally-length analysis, scenario patterns (CC BY-NC-SA 4.0)
  • Open-Meteo: live weather and 3-day forecast for tournament cities (free, no API key)

09What the Model Doesn't Know

This is the honest section. Things the model can't see:

  • Injuries. Match data shows the result, not the wrist taping or the back tightness that drove it.
  • Travel and time-zone fatigue. We have a basic fatigue proxy from match load, but not flight schedules.
  • Personal events. A player with a sick child or a coaching split is not flagged anywhere in the data.
  • Walkover-inflated ratings. A player who advanced through 3 retirements has a Glicko score that may not reflect their match form.
  • Coaching changes. The model learns from how a player has played, not who is courtside today.

10Limitations

  • ATP only. No WTA, no doubles. Sackmann maintains a parallel tennis_wta repo we plan to ingest.
  • Pre-1991 matches lack serve/return stats. Match-result percentiles cover the full 1968–today window; serve stats only go back to 1991.
  • Match Charting Project coverage is uneven. Top players have hundreds of charted matches; lower-ranked or older players may have fewer than 5. Volley and footwork attributes show "Insufficient data" when the sample is thin.
  • Live tournament results lag the scraper. tennis-data.co.uk updates once a day, so results within a tournament can trail real time by 24 hours.
